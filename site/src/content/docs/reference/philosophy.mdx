Kraken's design decisions are grounded in adversarially verified research:
Anthropic's multi-agent system write-ups, Cognition's context principles,
Uber's SubmitQueue, the MAST failure taxonomy, and AgenticFlict conflict
data. They compress into four sentences.

## Contracts, not vibes

Work enters the fleet as a [typed contract](../../concepts/contracts/):
one objective, explicit owned paths, a verifiable expected state. This is
not ceremony for its own sake. Every hard guarantee downstream (boundary
enforcement, judge rubrics, conflict radar, findings routing) hangs off
those fields. A fleet coordinated by prompt prose has nothing to
verify against; a fleet coordinated by contracts does. The same discipline
binds the human: your goal statement is the planner's entire world, so
intent, constraints, and done-criteria go in writing.

## Verification over trust

Agents report success; Kraken checks. Gates run as processes, not opinions.
The [judge](../../concepts/judge/) scores end states against contracts,
treats gate evidence as outranking its own impression, and holds the same
tool grants as the worker, so "I posted the PR comment" is checked against
`gh`, not against confidence. Claims that can't be evidenced don't pass, and
a failure to parse a verdict fails closed. The trunk itself isn't trusted:
it's probed against the gates before any fleet work is allowed to merge.

## The journal never lies

Every fact is an [append-only event](../../concepts/journal/). Nothing
mutates, so nothing can be quietly rewritten: who decided what (a human, a
connected agent session, or Kraken itself) is permanent record, auto-heals journal the
question they answered themselves, and every commit on trunk traces back
through verdict, gates, contract, and approved plan to the goal that started
it. Resume, audit, and the entire bridge UI are projections of the same
sequence; state that can't drift from history, because it *is* history.

## Everything that didn't need you happens without you

Mechanical failures [heal themselves](../../concepts/auto-heal/) on a
budget, with progress detection. Stale decisions
[resolve themselves](../../concepts/journal/#decisions-auto-invalidate) when
reality moves on. Green waves merge without asking. What reaches your
[queue](../../concepts/decisions/) and your lock screen is the residue
that genuinely needs judgment: plans, behavioral doubt, permission grants,
stalls. Approval fatigue is a design failure, not a user failure; a system
that asks about everything trains you to approve anything.

## What Kraken is not

Honesty cuts both ways. Kraken deliberately does **not** build:

<div class="k-prompt-list">
- **An IDE agent.** Kraken doesn't pair with you in an editor and has no conversational coding loop. It's the layer *behind* your agents: use Claude Code or Codex for the conversation, Kraken for landing the results.
- **An agent harness.** No model calls, no context management, no tool-use implementation. [Arms](../../concepts/arms/) are the harnesses you already run.
- **A CI system.** Gates are 2–3-minute tripwires on the merge path, not your build matrix. Keep CI; Kraken's train decides what reaches the branch CI watches.
- **Model routing, review UIs, or sandboxing.** Use LiteLLM, use Crit/Plannotator, inherit the harness's sandbox. Kraken composes with these; it doesn't clone them.
</div>

And one caveat worth stating plainly: **the judge is a strong filter, not an
oracle.** It's one rubric call over gates, diff, and report: cheap by
design, hard-ruled where it can be, and tool-equipped to verify side effects.
It will catch confident garbage far more often than no judge; it will not
catch everything a senior reviewer would. That's why behavioral doubt always
escalates to a human, why gates stay honest and fast, and why an adversarial
reviewer can be [made a gate](../../start/onboard/#the-gates-philosophy-fast-honest-tripwires)
when the stakes warrant it.
